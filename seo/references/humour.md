# Humour - Grout Academy

> Read this file before writing any blog or service-page content. The decision below is the most important voice call for this brand.

## The decision: no humour

Grout Academy does not do humour in client-facing copy. No dad jokes, no parenthetical asides, no "(yes, we know how that sounds)" winks, no pop-culture references.

This is not an omission. It is a deliberate brand choice and it must be respected on every page.

## Why

The brand sells trust in a category where the customer is letting a stranger into their bathroom or backyard for one to two days, replacing materials they will live with for a decade. The reader is not in the mood for a joke. They have mould creeping back, a wall that's gone soft, or a pool coping that is starting to lift. They want a calm, capable, plain-spoken answer.

Aaron stays anonymous on the site. The voice is the business, not a personality. Personality voices (Marco the dad-joking plumber, the tradie with the YouTube channel) work when the personality is doing the selling. Anonymous + jokes reads as forced. Anonymous + plain reads as competent.

The existing site already commits to this register. The closest the brand gets to a flourish is "the work speaks louder than words" on the homepage. That is the upper bound. Do not try to be funnier than the brand.

## What replaces humour

The same things humour does for Marco the plumber - keeping the reader engaged, breaking up dense information, signalling a human wrote this - have to be done by other means here.

- **Specifics replace jokes.** Where Marco would land a pun, Grout Academy lands a number, a brand name, or a concrete consequence. "Most standard residential shower regrouts in Geelong fall in a predictable range" is more memorable than a joke about grout.
- **Plain straight talk replaces self-deprecation.** Marco punches at himself; Grout Academy explains the trade-off. "If your tiles are cracked across most of the bathroom, you might need a re-tile, not a regrout" is the equivalent move - it's the brand telling the customer the truth at its own expense.
- **Short sentences replace parenthetical asides.** White space and rhythm carry the load that asides would carry in a personality voice.

## Anti-patterns - never do these

- **Exclamation marks.** Anywhere. Ever. The voice is calm, not excited.
- **Emoji icons.** In any context.
- **"Don't worry, we won't bore you with the technical details"** type meta-asides. The brand is the technical details.
- **Cute headings.** "Tile and tested" / "Grout and about" / "Sealing the deal" - never. Headings are statements of fact.
- **First-person warmth.** "We love what we do" / "Passionate about regrouting" - both veer toward the personality voice the brand specifically rejects.
- **Quirky copy in CTAs.** "Hit us up", "Drop us a line", "Slide into our DMs" - never. Standard CTAs are "Get a Free Quote" and "Call 0418 958 131".

## The vibe check

Before shipping, read the page silently and ask: does this sound like:

- A trusted tradie at the front door, walking a homeowner through what's wrong and what we'll do about it - **target**
- A brochure - **wrong direction, but better than the next two**
- A cheeky tradie on Instagram - **wrong**
- An AI trying to sound friendly - **wrong**

If the page reads as cheeky, dial it down. If it reads as brochure, add specifics (numbers, brand names, concrete consequences) until it reads as a tradesman.

## When humour rules might change

The brand's humour rule could shift if:
- Aaron decides to put his name and face on the site (changes the voice from anonymous-business to person-with-personality)
- A blog series or social channel is launched specifically aimed at homeowner education and the tone needs to be lighter to compete for attention
- A different sub-brand is created for a different audience (commercial vs residential, for example)

None of these are in play. Until one of them is, this rule stands.
